# Aircraft-Predictive-Maintenance-Project

At a global scale, the Aviation industry is one of those industries that require utmost precision and accuracy. It was a good learning experience for me to learn more about it and work on the problems associated with it, so that I can get a holistic idea of the things.
In 2018, Indian aviation faced a massive crisis. Major carriers like IndiGo and GoAir were forced to ground dozens of their brand-new Airbus A320neo aircraft. The issue? The Pratt & Whitney 1100G engines were failing prematurely due to a 'combustor seal' and 'bearing' issue.
From a business perspective, this was a nightmare. A grounded plane costs an airline roughly $150,000 per day in lost revenue. However, if an engine fails mid-flight, the cost isn't just financial—it’s a matter of safety and brand trust. The airlines were stuck: they couldn't just replace every engine (too expensive/no spare parts), but they couldn't risk flying them either.
The traditional maintenance approach failed here. Engines were supposed to last thousands of hours, but these were failing at just 500 hours. Scheduled maintenance was useless because the 'cycle' of failure was unpredictable. What the airlines needed was Predictive Maintenance—a way to look at the 'vitals' (sensor data) and say: 'This specific engine is showing the signature of a failure within the next 30 cycles'.
This motivated me to work on a project like this to understand the cutting-edge precision and accuracy in these types of industries. And this is exactly the problem I addressed in my project using the NASA CMAPSS dataset. I built a deep-learning model using LSTM networks and compared it with different models to move away from 'guesses' and toward 'data-driven alerts.'

Instead of asking 'How long does a generic engine last?', my model looks at 21 different sensors (like EGT and fuel flow) to provide a Binary Risk Assessment. By optimizing the decision threshold to maximize the F1-Score, I ensured the model was sensitive enough to catch these early-stage failures (Safety) while minimizing 'False Alarms' that lead to unnecessary groundings (Profitability).

The idea is that the business moves from Reactive (fixing it when it breaks) to Proactive. In the case of the Indian aviation crisis, a model like this would have allowed airlines to pull only the specific 'at-risk' engines for repair overnight, keeping the rest of the fleet in the air and saving millions in operational disruptions.
